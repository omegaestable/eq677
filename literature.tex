\begin{abstract}
The Equational Theories Project leaves open, up to duality, the finite implication
\(\E{677}\models_{\fin}\E{255}\) for magmas, while the corresponding unrestricted
implication is known to be false.  This note records a checked package of reductions for
that remaining finite problem.  The main output is a route graph: the target law at a
point is equivalent to a unique right fixed point, to a left-division identity called
Lemma L, and to several constrained collision and counterexample conditions.  The note
also isolates false trails that should not be reused, including a previously suspected
quotient family and a full-shift law.  No proof or finite counterexample is claimed.
\end{abstract}

Primary 08A05; Secondary 08B05, 03C13, 68V15.

Finite magma, equational law, finite implication, left division, equational theories.

\section{Introduction}

A \emph{magma} is a set \(M\) equipped with a binary operation \(\op\).  The two laws
studied here are
\begin{align*}
\E{677}:&\qquad x=y\op\bigl(x\op((y\op x)\op y)\bigr),\\
\E{255}:&\qquad x=((x\op x)\op x)\op x.
\end{align*}
The problem is the finite implication
\[
   \E{677}\models_{\fin}\E{255},
\]
that is, whether every finite magma satisfying \(\E{677}\) also satisfies \(\E{255}\).
The unrestricted implication \(\E{677}\models\E{255}\) is false.  The Equational Theories
Project (ETP) paper records the finite problem as the only unresolved finite implication
up to duality, equivalently together with the dual implication
\(\E{2910}\models_{\fin}\E{47}\), and tentatively conjectures the implication to be
false \cite[Problem 8.1]{ETPPaper}.  The ETP blueprint chapter on Equation 677 contains
the local structure theory and the known no-go statements for standard counterexample
families \cite{ETP677}; the dashboard records the graph status \cite{ETPDashboard}.

This note is not a computational search report.  Its purpose is to make the remaining
mathematical problem sharper.  The reductions below are organized around a fixed element
\(x\) of a finite \(\E{677}\)-magma.  They show that the target law at \(x\) is the same
as a single right fixed point, that any such fixed-point witness is unique, and that the
same target can be expressed as a left-division identity (Lemma L).  The complementary
sections describe what a collision proof or a finite counterexample would have to supply.

\section{Preliminaries and external input}

For \(a\in M\), write
\[
   L_a(t)=a\op t,\qquad R_a(t)=t\op a,\qquad S(t)=t\op t.
\]
If \(L_a\) is bijective, write \(a\backslash b\) for the unique element \(u\) satisfying
\(a\op u=b\).  All products in this note are non-associative and are parenthesized when
there is any possible ambiguity.

The finite hypothesis first enters through the following standard ETP fact.

\begin{proposition}[Left division in finite \(\E{677}\)-magmas]\label{prop:left-division}
Let \(M\) be a finite magma satisfying \(\E{677}\).  Then every left translation \(L_a\)
is bijective.  Moreover
\[
   a\backslash b=b\op((a\op b)\op a)
\]
for all \(a,b\in M\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Law \(\E{677}\) gives
\[
   b=a\op\bigl(b\op((a\op b)\op a)\bigr).
\]
Thus \(L_a\) is surjective.  Since \(M\) is finite, \(L_a\) is bijective, and the displayed
preimage formula is exactly the formula for \(a\backslash b\).
\end{proof}

The ETP chapter also records several strategic facts that are used here only as external
input.  The unrestricted implication \(\E{677}\not\models\E{255}\) is witnessed by the
free \(\E{677}\)-magma \cite[Section 13.2]{ETP677}.  Linear models satisfying
\(\E{677}\) satisfy \(\E{255}\), and affine-fiber extensions over such models do not
produce counterexamples \cite[Lemmas 13.3--13.4]{ETP677}.  The same chapter gives finite
\(\E{677}\)-magmas that are not right-cancellative \cite[Section 13.1]{ETP677}.  Hence no
argument below may use right cancellation, associativity, an identity element, or group or
quasigroup intuition unless such structure has first been derived.  The finite-map lemmas
in the ETP finite chapter are a natural source of future tools \cite[Lemmas 5.5--5.7]{ETPFinite}.

\begin{lemma}[Transformed identity]\label{lem:transformed}
Let \(M\) be a finite magma satisfying \(\E{677}\).  Then
\[
   z=(y\op z)\op((y\op(y\op z))\op y)
\]
for all \(y,z\in M\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Substitute \(y\op z\) for \(x\) in \(\E{677}\).  This gives
\[
   y\op z
   =y\op\bigl((y\op z)\op((y\op(y\op z))\op y)\bigr).
\]
Cancel the common left factor \(y\) using Proposition \ref{prop:left-division}.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Key identity]\label{lem:key-identity}
Let \(M\) be a finite magma satisfying \(\E{677}\).  Then
\[
   (y\op(y\op z))\op y
   =z\op(((y\op z)\op z)\op(y\op z))
\]
for all \(y,z\in M\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Lemma \ref{lem:transformed} gives
\[
   z=(y\op z)\op((y\op(y\op z))\op y).
\]
On the other hand, applying \(\E{677}\) with the parameter \(y\op z\) gives
\[
   z=(y\op z)\op\bigl(z\op(((y\op z)\op z)\op(y\op z))\bigr).
\]
Left cancellation by \(y\op z\) gives the identity.
\end{proof}

\section{The orbit package}

Fix a finite \(\E{677}\)-magma \(M\) and an element \(x\in M\).  Define
\[
   c_0=x,\qquad c_{k+1}=x\op c_k,
\]
and let \(d\) be the minimal period of this \(L_x\)-orbit, so that \(c_d=x\) and
\(c_0,\ldots,c_{d-1}\) are pairwise distinct.  Indices on the \(c_k\) are read modulo
\(d\).  Put
\[
   d_k=c_k\op x.
\]

\begin{lemma}[Orbit recurrence]\label{lem:orbit-recurrence}
For every \(k\),
\[
   c_{k-1}=c_k\op d_{k+1}.
\]
Consequently \(d_1=c_{d-2}\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Apply \(\E{677}\) with \(y=x\) and the law variable \(x\) replaced by \(c_k\):
\[
   c_k=x\op\bigl(c_k\op((x\op c_k)\op x)\bigr)
       =x\op(c_k\op d_{k+1}).
\]
Since also \(c_k=x\op c_{k-1}\), left cancellation gives the recurrence.  Taking
\(k=0\) gives \(c_{d-1}=x\op d_1\), so \(d_1=x\backslash c_{d-1}=c_{d-2}\).
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Basic left-division identities]\label{lem:division-identities}
For every \(k\),
\[
   x\backslash c_k=c_{k-1},\qquad c_k\backslash c_{k-1}=d_{k+1}.
\]
If
\[
   p=x\backslash x=c_{d-1},\qquad q=x\backslash p,
\]
then
\[
   q=d_1=c_{d-2}=(x\op x)\op x,
\]
and
\[
   p\op(x\op x)=d_1,
   \qquad p\backslash d_1=x\op x.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The first identity follows from \(c_k=x\op c_{k-1}\).  The second is precisely the
recurrence in Lemma \ref{lem:orbit-recurrence}.  Since \(x\op c_{d-1}=c_d=x\), one has
\(p=c_{d-1}\), and then
\[
   q=x\backslash p=x\backslash c_{d-1}=c_{d-2}=d_1.
\]
Also \(d_1=c_1\op x=(x\op x)\op x\).  Finally, applying the general identity
\(a\backslash(a\backslash b)=(a\backslash b)\op(b\op a)\), which follows immediately
from Proposition \ref{prop:left-division}, with \(a=b=x\), gives
\[
   x\backslash p=p\op(x\op x).
\]
The left side is \(q=d_1\), and hence \(p\op(x\op x)=d_1\).  The last displayed identity
is the definition of left division by \(p\).
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[A fixed point has the forced index]\label{lem:forced-index}
If \(d_j=x\), then \(j\equiv d-2\pmod d\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The condition \(d_j=x\) says \(c_j\op x=x\).  Lemma \ref{lem:transformed} with
\(y=c_j,z=x\) gives
\[
   x=x\op(x\op c_j).
\]
On the other hand, \(\E{677}\) with both variables specialized to \(x\) gives
\[
   x=x\op(x\op d_1).
\]
Two left cancellations yield \(c_j=d_1=c_{d-2}\), so the minimality of the orbit period
gives the claimed congruence.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[No two-cycle in the \(L_x\)-orbit]\label{cor:no-two-cycle}
The period \(d\) is never equal to \(2\).  If \(d=1\), then \(\EqAt{\E{255}}{x}\)
holds.  Hence a counterexample point must have \(d\geq 3\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
If \(d=1\), then \(x\op x=x\), and so
\[
   ((x\op x)\op x)\op x=(x\op x)\op x=x\op x=x.
\]
If \(d=2\), Lemma \ref{lem:orbit-recurrence} gives \(d_1=c_{d-2}=c_0=x\).  But
Lemma \ref{lem:forced-index} applied with \(j=1\) would give
\(1\equiv d-2\equiv 0\pmod 2\), a contradiction.
\end{proof}

\section{The route graph}

The following display is the dependency graph used in the rest of the note.  The first
row contains equivalent fixed-point targets.  The two lower branches describe the two
ways an unfinished proof can still move: collision lifting or explicit counterexample
construction.
\[
\begin{array}{cccccc}
\EqAt{\E{255}}{x}
&\Longleftrightarrow& q\op x=x
&\Longleftrightarrow& \exists u\ (u\op x=x)
&\Longleftrightarrow q\backslash x=x \\
&& \Updownarrow &&& \Updownarrow \\
&& \text{unique witness }q
&&& ((x\op x)\op((x\op x)\op x))\op(x\op x)=x \\
&&&&& \Updownarrow \\
&&&&& (q\op x)\op q=p .
\end{array}
\]
If the fixed point is not established, the two honest remaining branches are
\[
\begin{array}{ccccc}
d_i=d_j=e
&\Longrightarrow& (c_i\op e)\op c_i=(c_j\op e)\op c_j
&\dashrightarrow& \text{finite counting pressure on }y\mapsto y\op e,\\[2mm]
x\notin\operatorname{im}R_x
&\Longrightarrow& y\op x=z\op x=r
&\Longrightarrow& (y\op r)\op y=r\backslash x.
\end{array}
\]
The dashed arrow is a missing lemma, not a theorem; the collision branch is now best
viewed as a counting problem rather than a direct propagation problem.

\section{The fixed-point frontier}

The fixed-point route is sharper than the existential formulation suggests.

\begin{proposition}[Unique right fixed-point witness]\label{prop:unique-witness}
Let \(M\) be a finite \(\E{677}\)-magma and fix \(x\in M\).  If \(u\op x=x\), then
\[
   u=q=(x\op x)\op x.
\]
Consequently \(\EqAt{\E{255}}{x}\) is equivalent to the existence of \(u\) with
\(u\op x=x\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The first half of the proof is the argument already used in Lemma \ref{lem:forced-index}.
If \(u\op x=x\), then Lemma \ref{lem:transformed} gives
\[
   x=x\op(x\op u).
\]
The specialization \(y=x\), \(x=x\) in \(\E{677}\) gives
\[
   x=x\op(x\op q).
\]
Two left cancellations give \(u=q\).  Since \(q=(x\op x)\op x\), the identity
\(q\op x=x\) is exactly \(\EqAt{\E{255}}{x}\).  Thus \(\E{255}\) at \(x\) is equivalent
to the existence of a right fixed point over \(x\), and the witness is forced to be \(q\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Equivalent local targets]\label{prop:local-targets}
With the notation above, the following are equivalent:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \(\EqAt{\E{255}}{x}\), equivalently \(q\op x=x\);
\item \(q\backslash x=x\);
\item \(((x\op x)\op((x\op x)\op x))\op(x\op x)=x\);
\item \((q\op x)\op q=p\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The equivalence of (1) and (2) is just uniqueness of left division by \(q\).  To identify
the left quotient, apply Lemma \ref{lem:transformed} with \(y=x\op x\) and \(z=x\):
\[
   x=q\op\bigl(((x\op x)\op q)\op(x\op x)\bigr).
\]
Therefore
\[
   q\backslash x=((x\op x)\op q)\op(x\op x),
\]
which proves the equivalence of (2) and (3), because \(q=(x\op x)\op x\).

For (4), use Proposition \ref{prop:left-division}:
\[
   q\backslash x=x\op((q\op x)\op q).
\]
Since \(p=x\backslash x\), injectivity of \(L_x\) gives
\[
   x\op((q\op x)\op q)=x
   \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
   (q\op x)\op q=p.
\]
Together with (1)\(\Longleftrightarrow\)(2), this proves the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Lemma L]\label{def:lemma-l}
For the fixed element \(x\), write \(\mathrm{L}(x)\) for the identity
\[
   ((x\op x)\op((x\op x)\op x))\op(x\op x)=x.
\]
By Proposition \ref{prop:local-targets}, \(\mathrm{L}(x)\) is equivalent, in the present
finite \(\E{677}\) setting, to \(\EqAt{\E{255}}{x}\).
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
Lemma L is a target, not an established lemma.  Because the unrestricted implication
\(\E{677}\models\E{255}\) is false \cite[Section 13.2]{ETP677}, any successful proof of
the fixed-point route must use finiteness in a visible way: for example through left
bijection, orbit periodicity, a finite-map lemma, or a collision argument.  A derivation
that simply rewrites \(\E{677}\) into Lemma L without using finite structure should be
treated as suspect.
\end{remark}

\begin{lemma}[Fixed-map and edge-product form]\label{lem:fixed-map}
For the fixed element \(x\), define
\[
   H_x(t)=((x\op t)\op x),
   \qquad
   F_x(t)=x\op(t\op x).
\]
Then
\[
   x\backslash t=t\op H_x(t) \tag{E}
\]
for every \(t\in M\), and
\[
   F_x(L_x(t))=L_x(H_x(t)). \tag{Conj}
\]
Consequently \(F_x\) and \(H_x\) have the same finite cycle structure.  Moreover
\(\EqAt{\E{255}}{x}\) is equivalent to either of these maps having a fixed point.
Thus, at a counterexample point \(x\), both maps are fixed-point-free and every
cycle of either map has length at least \(2\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Apply \(\E{677}\) with parameter \(x\) and law variable \(t\):
\[
   t=x\op\bigl(t\op((x\op t)\op x)\bigr)=x\op(t\op H_x(t)).
\]
This is exactly (E).  The conjugacy identity is immediate:
\[
   F_x(L_x(t))=x\op((x\op t)\op x)=L_x(H_x(t)).
\]
Since \(L_x\) is bijective, (Conj) gives the same cycle structure.

If \(F_x(z)=z\), then
\[
   x\op(z\op x)=z.
\]
The left-inverse formula for \(L_x\), applied to \(z\), also gives
\[
   x\op\bigl(z\op((x\op z)\op x)\bigr)=z.
\]
Left cancellation by \(x\), then by \(z\), yields \((x\op z)\op x=x\).  Hence a
right fixed-point witness over \(x\) exists, and Proposition \ref{prop:unique-witness}
gives \(\EqAt{\E{255}}{x}\).  Conversely, if \(u\op x=x\), set
\[
   z=u\op((x\op u)\op x).
\]
Then \(x\op z=u\) by \(\E{677}\) with parameter \(x\), and another use of the same
identity gives \(z=x\op(z\op x)\), so \(F_x(z)=z\).  Finally, fixed points of
\(F_x\) and \(H_x\) correspond through the bijection \(L_x\) by (Conj).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Identity (E) turns the fixed-point route into a finite dynamics problem.  If
\(H_x(t)=t\), then \(x\backslash t=t\op t\), equivalently \(L_x(S(t))=t\), which is
the last fixed-point form in the ETP Lemma 13.2 list.  In a bad point, the forward
orbit of any element under \(H_x\) must eventually enter a cycle of length greater
than \(1\).  A finite proof via this route would have to show that such an
\(H_x\)-cycle is incompatible with the edge products \(x\backslash t=t\op H_x(t)\).
\end{remark}

\section{Collision frontier}

The orbit recurrence suggests a collision strategy, but the exact missing step is narrow.

\begin{proposition}[Collision lift]\label{prop:collision-lift}
If \(d_i=d_j=e\), then
\[
   (c_i\op e)\op c_i=(c_j\op e)\op c_j. \tag{C}
\]
If in addition \(i\not\equiv j\pmod d\), then
\[
   c_i\op e\neq c_j\op e.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Lemma \ref{lem:transformed} with \(y=c_k\) and \(z=x\) gives
\[
   x=d_k\op((c_k\op d_k)\op c_k).
\]
If \(d_i=d_j=e\), left cancellation by \(e\) yields (C).  If also
\(c_i\op e=c_j\op e=s\), then (C) becomes \(s\op c_i=s\op c_j\).  Left cancellation by
\(s\) gives \(c_i=c_j\), contradicting the minimal period unless
\(i\equiv j\pmod d\).
\end{proof}

\begin{problem}[Collision-counting target]\label{prob:collision-target}
Turn a nontrivial collision \(d_i=d_j=e\) into a finite counting contradiction, or into
the fixed point \(q\op x=x\).  More precisely, let
\[
   F_e=\{y\in M:y\op x=e\},
   \qquad
   A_e(y)=y\op e.
\]
Proposition \ref{prop:collision-lift}, and more generally Proposition
\ref{prop:rectangles}, show that \(A_e\) is injective on \(F_e\).  Thus the old direct
target \(c_i\op e=c_j\op e\) is not a plausible standalone propagation lemma for a
genuine collision: it is precisely what the splitter forbids unless a separate argument
has already produced the fixed point.  A collision proof should instead find a natural
finite set \(B_e\) such that
\[
   A_e(F_e)\subseteq B_e
\]
and \(|B_e|<|F_e|\), unless \(q\op x=x\).
\end{problem}

The common shortcut ``prove \(k\mapsto d_k\) injective'' is not by itself a proof of the
main theorem.  It becomes useful only if the argument also connects the resulting
injectivity to the forced fixed point \(q\op x=x\), or if it supplies the counting
pressure requested in Problem \ref{prob:collision-target}.

\section{Counterexample frontier}

Suppose a finite counterexample exists and choose \(x\in M\) with
\(q\op x\neq x\).  Proposition \ref{prop:unique-witness} says that no element \(u\) has
\(u\op x=x\).  Thus \(x\notin\operatorname{im}R_x\).  Since \(M\) is finite, \(R_x\) is
not injective, and there exist \(y\neq z\) and \(r\neq x\) such that
\[
   y\op x=z\op x=r.
\]
The following proposition records the forced shape of such a right collision.

\begin{proposition}[Right-collision rectangles]\label{prop:rectangles}
Let \(y\op x=r\).  Then
\[
   (y\op r)\op y=r\backslash x. \tag{R}
\]
Consequently, if \(y\op x=z\op x=r\) and \(y\op r=z\op r\), then \(y=z\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Lemma \ref{lem:transformed} with \(y=y\) and \(z=x\) gives
\[
   x=r\op((y\op r)\op y),
\]
which is exactly (R).  If \(y\op r=z\op r=s\), then (R) gives \(s\op y=s\op z\).  Left
cancellation by \(s\) gives \(y=z\).
\end{proof}

This proposition is a counterexample design constraint.  A candidate finite counterexample
must contain right-collision fibers for every bad point \(x\), but those fibers must split
under the second probe \(t\mapsto t\op r\), while all elements in the fiber preserve the
same twisted diagonal value \((t\op r)\op t=r\backslash x\).

There are two plausible construction languages that respect this constraint.  First,
because all left translations are permutations, one may write
\[
   a\op b=\sigma(a)(b)
\]
with \(\sigma(a)\in\operatorname{Sym}(M)\).  Law \(\E{677}\) is then the labeled-
permutation condition
\[
   \sigma(y)\bigl(\sigma(x)(\sigma(\sigma(y)(x))(y))\bigr)=x.
\]
A counterexample at \(x_0\) would additionally require \(\sigma(a)(x_0)\neq x_0\) for
every label \(a\).  This is not merely a group-theoretic condition on a subgroup of
\(\operatorname{Sym}(M)\); the labeling map \(a\mapsto\sigma(a)\) is part of the data.
In fact this labeling map is injective.  If \(\sigma(y)=\sigma(z)\), choose any
\(x\) and put \(e=y\op x=z\op x\).  The two specializations of \(\E{677}\) give
\[
   x=y\op(x\op(e\op y)),
   \qquad
   x=z\op(x\op(e\op z)).
\]
Since \(L_y=L_z\), the first equality may be rewritten with left factor \(z\).  Left
cancellation by \(z\), then by \(x\), and finally by \(e\), gives \(y=z\).  Thus a
counterexample in this language is an injectively labeled set of permutations.  In
particular, if \(y\op x=z\op x=r\) with \(y\neq z\), the two distinct permutations
\(\sigma(y),\sigma(z)\) agree at \(x\) but must separate at \(r\):
\[
   \sigma(y)(r)\neq \sigma(z)(r),
   \qquad
   \sigma(\sigma(y)(r))(y)=r\backslash x=
   \sigma(\sigma(z)(r))(z).
\]

Second, one may seek nonlinear fiber extensions over positive \(\E{677}\)-models.  The
ETP affine-extension obstruction \cite[Lemma 13.4]{ETP677} rules out the obvious affine
version, so a successful extension would have to be genuinely nonlinear and would still
need to realize the rectangles in Proposition \ref{prop:rectangles}.

\section{Obstructions and false routes}

Several attractive shortcuts are invalid or unsupported.

\begin{enumerate}
\item The family
\[
   d_k\backslash x=c_{k-2}\op c_{k-1}
\]
should not be used.  The old derivation confused \(c_{k-1}\op x\) with \(d_k\); by
definition it is \(d_{k-1}\).  The active repository does not contain a publication-ready
finite table witness for this failure, so the present paper records only the derivation
error and quarantines the identity.
\item The full-shift law
\[
   p\op d_k=d_{k+1}
\]
is also quarantined.  It is not a consequence of the trusted local package.
\item A proof of universal idempotence, \(d=1\), or any universal small-period conclusion
would contradict known nontrivial positive models from the ETP finite theory.  Such a
claim should therefore be audited before it is used.
\item A nontrivial collision \(d_i=d_j\) does not automatically propagate backwards to
\(d_{i-1}=d_{j-1}\).  Any cascade proof must derive the propagation or avoid it.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Open frontiers}

The work above leaves three precise frontiers.

\begin{problem}[Fixed-point route]
Prove \(q\op x=x\), equivalently Lemma L from Definition \ref{def:lemma-l}, by an
argument that uses finite structure explicitly.
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Collision route]
Resolve Problem \ref{prob:collision-target}: from \(d_i=d_j=e\), derive either the
fixed point \(q\op x=x\) or a finite counting contradiction for the injective splitter
\(A_e(y)=y\op e\) on the fiber \(F_e=\{y:y\op x=e\}\).
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Counterexample route]
Construct a finite labeled-permutation model or a genuinely nonlinear fiber extension
satisfying \(\E{677}\), violating \(\E{255}\), and satisfying the right-collision
rectangle constraints of Proposition \ref{prop:rectangles}.  A table-free parameter
search is not a construction; any proposed example should include a complete finite table
or a checkable structural verification.
\end{problem}

\section{Proof audit checklist}

The reductions in this note use left cancellation only after Proposition
\ref{prop:left-division}, where finiteness has made each \(L_a\) bijective.  They do not
use right cancellation, associativity, an identity element, or group/quasigroup structure.
Future work on this problem should state which of the three frontiers it attacks and end,
if unfinished, with one missing lemma rather than a broad search prescription.

Every new proof attempt should check the following list before being added to the
mathematical record.
\begin{enumerate}
\item All products are parenthesized.
\item Every cancellation is a left cancellation by a known bijective left translation.
\item Every use of finiteness is named: left bijectivity, orbit periodicity, a finite-map
lemma, or pigeonhole reasoning for a self-map.
\item The false quotient family and false full-shift law are not used.
\item Any counterexample claim contains either a complete finite operation or a structural
verification of \(\E{677}\) and failure of \(\E{255}\).
\end{enumerate}
