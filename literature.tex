\begin{abstract}
This note consolidates the useful mathematical state of the repository after abandoning
the computational finite-model program.  The unrestricted implication
\(\E{677}\Rightarrow\E{255}\) is false, while the finite implication remains open in the
Equational Theories Project.  The purpose of the note is not to report another search, but
to isolate the identities, obstructions, and proof/counterexample routes that still look
mathematically live.
\end{abstract}

\section{Problem and status}

Let \((M,\op)\) be a magma.  The two laws are
\[
\E{677}:\qquad
x = y\op\bigl(x\op((y\op x)\op y)\bigr),
\]
and
\[
\E{255}:\qquad
x = ((x\op x)\op x)\op x.
\]
The question in this dossier is:
\[
\E{677}\models_{\fin}\E{255}\,?
\]
The finite hypothesis is essential.  The ETP hard-implications chapter records the
unrestricted implication \(\E{677}\not\models\E{255}\), while the finite section lists
``Does 677 imply 255?'' among the remaining open finite questions
\cite[Chapter 27]{ETPBlueprintHard}.  The ETP paper states the same open problem
explicitly and notes that it resisted the major project constructions
\cite[Problem 8.1]{ETPPaper}.  The ETP dashboard currently reports that the general
implication graph is complete, but the finite graph has two remaining proof gaps,
corresponding to this problem and its dual \cite{ETPDashboard}.

\section{External mathematical base}

The main external source is Chapter 13 of the ETP blueprint \cite{ETP677}.  It studies
finite \(\E{677}\)-magmas and records the following facts.

\begin{itemize}
\item With \(L_y(z)=y\op z\), \(R_y(z)=z\op y\), and \(Sx=x\op x\), law \(\E{677}\) can be
written as
\[
x=L_yL_xL_{L_yx}y.
\]
\item In a finite \(\E{677}\)-magma every left translation \(L_y\) is bijective.  The
inverse is
\[
L_y^{-1}(x)=x\op((y\op x)\op y).
\]
Thus left cancellation and left division are available.
\item If \(y\op x=x\), then \(y=(Sx)\op x\).  Consequently \(\E{255}\) at \(x\) is
equivalent to solvability of \(y\op x=x\).
\item Lemma 13.2 gives several equivalent fixed-point formulations, including
solvability of \(R_x y=x\), \(R_xL_x z=x\), \(L_xR_x z=z\), \(R_xL_x y=y\), and
\(L_xS y=y\).
\item Linear and affine-fiber extension families do not give counterexamples
\cite[Lemmas 13.3 and 13.4]{ETP677}.  This matters because any counterexample route
must be non-linear or otherwise evade those no-go mechanisms.
\item Chapter 13.1 gives a finite \(\E{677}\)-magma that is not right-cancellative
\cite[Section 13.1]{ETP677}.  This warns against importing right-cancellative or
quasigroup intuition.
\end{itemize}

The classical background is useful mainly for orientation.  Austin's finite-model work
\cite{Austin1966} and Kisielewicz's Austin-identity work \cite{Kisielewicz1997} explain
why finite-vs-infinite behavior can be genuinely subtle for single identities.  Knuth's
central-groupoid work \cite{Knuth1970} and Knuth--Bendix completion
\cite{KnuthBendix1970} are relevant precedents for equational laws whose finite and free
objects behave very differently.  Baader--Nipkow \cite{BaaderNipkow1998}, Willsey et al.
\cite{Egg2021}, and Koehler et al. \cite{GuidedEqSat2024} are context for rewriting and
equality saturation, but they should not be treated as a license to restart blind
computation here.

\section{Trusted local package}

Fix \(x\in M\), where \(M\) is finite and satisfies \(\E{677}\).  Define
\[
c_0=x,\qquad c_{k+1}=x\op c_k.
\]
Let \(d\) be the minimal period of this \(L_x\)-orbit, so \(c_d=x\) and
\(c_0,\ldots,c_{d-1}\) are pairwise distinct.  Define
\[
d_k=c_k\op x.
\]
Use left division \(a\backslash b\) for the unique element \(u\) with \(a\op u=b\).

The following package is safe to use.

\begin{enumerate}
\item Left translations are bijective, so left cancellation is valid.
\item The transformed identity
\[
z=(y\op z)\op((y\op(y\op z))\op y)
\]
is obtained by substituting \(x=y\op z\) into \(\E{677}\) and left-cancelling \(y\).
\item The orbit recurrence
\[
c_{k-1}=c_k\op d_{k+1}
\]
follows from applying \(\E{677}\) with \(y=x\) and \(x=c_k\), then cancelling the outer
left multiplication by \(x\).
\item \(d_1=c_{d-2}\).
\item If \(d_j=x\), then \(j=d-2\pmod d\).
\item The quotient formula
\[
a\backslash b=b\op((a\op b)\op a)
\]
holds for all \(a,b\).
\item Safe local orbit identities include
\[
x\backslash c_k=c_{k-1},\qquad
c_k\backslash c_{k-1}=d_{k+1}.
\]
With \(p=x\backslash x=c_{d-1}\), also
\[
x\backslash p=d_1,\qquad p\backslash d_1=c_1,\qquad p\op c_1=d_1.
\]
\end{enumerate}

\section{Useful failures}

The following failures should be treated as mathematical information, not as loose
warnings.

\begin{itemize}
\item The quotient family
\[
d_k\backslash x=c_{k-2}\op c_{k-1}
\]
is false in tracked finite \(\E{677}\)-models that satisfy \(\E{255}\).  It arose from an
index slip: \(c_{k-1}\op x=d_{k-1}\), not \(d_k\).
\item The full shift law
\[
p\op d_k=d_{k+1},\qquad p=x\backslash x,
\]
is also false in tracked finite \(\E{677}\)-models.
\item Consequently, proving \(d=1\), universal idempotence, or a universal small-period
theorem for the \(L_x\)-orbit is the wrong target.  Known finite \(\E{677}\)-models can
have nontrivial orbit period and still satisfy \(\E{255}\).
\item Any claim that \(c_{d-2}\op x=x\) is already established should be audited.  This
identity is the clean fixed-point target; using it as an input risks proving the theorem by
renaming the theorem.
\item A collision \(d_i=d_j\) gives one recurrence step, but does not by itself imply
\(d_{i-1}=d_{j-1}\).  Cascade arguments must derive this propagation or avoid needing it.
\end{itemize}

\section{Live proof routes}

\subsection{Fixed-point route}

By the ETP equivalences, it is enough to prove that for every \(x\) there exists \(u\) with
\[
u\op x=x.
\]
The most natural local target is
\[
c_{d-2}\op x=x.
\]
If this target is proved, the finite implication follows immediately from Lemma 13.1 or
13.2 of the blueprint.  The difficulty is to produce the fixed point without assuming a
false transport law on the full \(d_k\)-sequence.

\subsection{Left-division route}

The element \(p=x\backslash x=c_{d-1}\) is the natural local actor because \(x\op p=x\).
The false global shift law says too much, but a weaker local identity involving only
\[
p,\quad d_1,\quad d_{d-1},\quad c_{d-2},\quad c_{d-1}
\]
may be enough.  A useful target shape is:
\[
u\op(c_t\op x)=x
\]
for a structurally forced \(u\) and \(t\), or an identity that makes one \(d_t\) equal to
\(x\).  Once any \(d_t=x\) is obtained, the uniqueness fact pins \(t=d-2\pmod d\).

\subsection{Collision route}

If the map \(k\mapsto d_k\) is injective on \(\mathbb Z/d\mathbb Z\), then \(\E{255}\)
holds at \(x\).  A contradiction route starts with \(d_i=d_j\), \(i\neq j\), and uses
\[
c_{k-1}=c_k\op d_{k+1}
\]
to compare backward steps along the \(c\)-orbit.  The known obstruction is that a single
collision does not automatically propagate to neighboring indices.  A successful proof must
show either propagation, or a different mechanism forcing a repeated \(c\)-value or a fixed
point \(d_t=x\).

\subsection{Counterexample route}

The ETP paper says this finite implication resisted the standard constructions
\cite[Problem 8.1]{ETPPaper}.  Chapter 13 also rules out linear and affine-extension
counterexamples in broad families \cite[Lemmas 13.3 and 13.4]{ETP677}.  Therefore a
counterexample attempt should be structural rather than exhaustive: it should explain how
the construction evades left-division fixed-point mechanisms, linearity, affine fibers, and
right-cancellative intuition.

\section{Protocol for future notes}

A future proof attempt should state its exact target and audit every use of cancellation.
Only left cancellation is available by default.  Every new identity must be derived
uniformly in its indices; agreement at a few orbit positions is not evidence.  If a route
does not finish, the note should end with a single missing lemma and a short explanation of
why that lemma would settle the route.

A future counterexample attempt should give the full finite operation, then verify
\(\E{677}\) and refute \(\E{255}\) by a direct algebraic argument or a complete finite table
check that is small enough to inspect.  It should not be another parameter sweep.
